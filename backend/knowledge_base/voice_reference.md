## Voice reference — Bernedette's own drafted templates

These are real templates Bernedette wrote herself for a batch of researched prospects. They are the gold-standard voice for cold outreach — match this shape, not a generic "cold email" shape.

### What makes these work (the pattern to copy)
1. **One real, specific, recent trigger** — a dollar figure, a project phase, a job posting, a named vendor selection. Never a vague "I saw your company is growing." The trigger is the entire hook.
2. **One sentence connecting the trigger to a real consequence** — why this specific stage/scale/complexity creates the need (e.g. "a lot of concurrent, high-visibility construction and aviation programs to govern well").
3. **One sentence on what FaithForge does, in plain terms** — no jargon stacking, no listing five services at once.
4. **Exactly one CTA, phrased as a soft, specific ask** — a low-pressure invite to compare notes, tied to their specific situation. Never "let me know if you're interested" (too passive) or a hard pitch (too aggressive). VARY the wording — do NOT reuse the same sentence every time. Across Bernedette's real drafts the ask shows up as "would a brief 15-minute call make sense to compare notes on…", "would it be useful to grab 15 minutes to discuss…", "open to a short call to see if there's a fit?", "would you be open to a brief call to discuss…". Same spirit, different words each time.
5. **Consistent sign-off**: "Bernedette Atong" / "FaithForge Technologies and Consulting" / "faithforgetech.com" — three short lines, nothing added.
6. Short. These emails run 3 tight paragraphs plus a sign-off — never padded.

### Voice notes
- These templates target named individuals with a real, verifiable trigger. When generating outreach for a lead in our own CRM, use whatever real signal exists in their account/notes/custom_fields (especially the pain_points field, which carries the imported "Trigger / Rationale") the same way — a specific fact, not a generic compliment.
- **The SHAPE transfers to any segment; the CONTENT follows the trigger.** These four examples all pitch PMO/program governance because these particular leads were capital programs, ERP rollouts, and new-builds — where that's the natural fit. Match the FaithForge angle to the lead's actual trigger: an ERP rollout → ERP program governance; a stalled or at-risk program → Project Rescue (Assessment / Rescue Sprint / Embedded Leadership); a new PMO hire → standing up the function. Never force a "PMO governance" pitch onto a trigger that doesn't call for it.
- If no strong specific trigger exists for a lead, do not fabricate one — fall back to the steady-delivery-execution angle already established elsewhere, but keep the same economy of language and single-CTA discipline shown here.
